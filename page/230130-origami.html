Modular Origami

Constructing origami is how I express myself artistically and develop problem-solving skills. A single sheet of paper can be transformed into many figures, and this never ceases to challenge my skill and accuracy. When I fold modular (multi-sheet) origami, I open myself to a myriad of models that I enjoy assembling and color-coordinating.

Attempting to construct complex origami requires patience in deciphering the symbols on origami diagrams, and repeated trial and error. It takes many attempts for me to fold a complex single-sheet model, and perfection is key to the crisp look of my finished product.

The origami model that best exemplifies the challenges of origami construction is the “Dragon's Egg” by Yuri Shumakov. Over and over when I first attempted this model, the sheet of paper I was folding refused to divide into 512 squares. Even when I succeeded, the next diagonal folds lacked precise divisions through the squares. Lacking success with the current specifications, I then scoured through YouTube videos and origami how-to sites in search of a solution. Noticing a simplified version, I refocused on this smaller egg, and my subsequent efforts were rewarded. Though I still have not fully succeeded in folding a “Dragon’s Egg,” I am no longer incapable of doing so, as I now know the quick creases that can be translated to the larger egg.

Modular origami requires that many "units" be assembled into a unified whole. This allows me to coordinate colors and evenly distribute them around my model. Blues that blend together, dark versus light colors – these are only a few of the options at my disposal. Though I did not invent the origami models I construct, I can still make any finished product unique through an endless combination of colors, limited only by my imagination.

In short, origami allows me to be both rigorously analytical and vigorously creative at the same time. And as someone who envisions a career in science, I think this is a highly desirable combination of traits to have.
