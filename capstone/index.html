Wildfires in the western United States are increasing in frequency and severity due to climate change and rising temperatures (Brown et al. 2023). Depending on the severity of the wildfire, the remaining tree species composition can change or convert to shrubland. Thus, it is important to understand which trees will survive over the next ten years post-fire to determine the successional trajectory of the forests.

The Fire and Tree Mortality Database (FTM) includes 169,843 records of individual trees across the United States that have experienced either wildfires or prescribed burns (Cansler et al. 2020). This database was acquired through the US Forest Service Research Data Archive. The data include the diameter and height of the tree as well as fire injuries sustained and beetle presence. The records also note whether the tree is alive or dead up to ten years post-fire. This database has been previously used to create predictive models of tree mortality after fire as seen in Cansler et al. (2020) and Shearman et al. (2023).

This project explores how beetle presence, crown scorch, and bark char influence tree mortality after wildfires throughout the western US. Two logistic regression models were created to predict how likely a conifer tree would be alive after five years post-fire. Visualizations of the effect of each variable can be found in the Graphs tab.
