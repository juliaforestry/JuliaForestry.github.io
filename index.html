Current Research

As an undergraduate research apprentice at the Museum of Vertebrate Zoology, I use geospatial techniques to map habitat ranges for new and existing amphibian species. In general, I begin by reviewing species distribution accounts and suitable habitat requirements. Then, I extract and merge habitat criteria from geographic data layers with known specimen locations. This method of range mapping identifies the areas of probable species occurrences. Additionally, when working with existing species, I continuously update and expand existing ranges due to new sightings of said species.


With the Museum’s Conservation Working Group, I am aggregating type locality coordinates with currently mapped protected areas to identify hotspots of new species discoveries. The time frame focus is from 2010 to 2020 to detect newly described species trends in the last decade. By determining these areas of interest, further recommendations could be made about subsequent species surveys and conservation actions.

In the future, I plan to investigate amphibian species occurrences in protected areas around the world. Specifically, I want to examine the distribution of rediscovered species in protected areas as well as the intersection of amphibian species ranges and land set aside for conservation.
